Vietnam heads into 2026 with contained inflation and decent economic growth — but the State
Bank of Vietnam (SBV) faces policy trade-offs that are unusually acute by recent standards.
The central bank's reaction function does not primarily target inflation; it targets exchange
rate stability. That distinction matters considerably for how monetary tightening, if it
occurs, will be implemented.

---

## Inflation: Benign but Not the Binding Constraint

Inflation dynamics heading into 2026 are relatively benign and largely cost-push rather than
demand-pull. Price pressures have been driven more by administered prices and FX pass-through
than by broad domestic demand. Headline inflation has been shaped by food, energy, and
administered components; core inflation appears consistent with a still-manageable domestic
cycle.

The implication: monetary tightening in 2026, if deployed, is more likely driven by FX
defense, funding conditions, and capital flow management than by a need to cool the economy.
Where tightening does occur, instruments are likely to be diverse — a combination of policy
rate adjustments, FX swaps, and administrative measures rather than a clean rate-hiking cycle.

---

## FX Stability as the Binding Constraint

Vietnam's FX reserves cover approximately three months of imports — a constraint that
materially limits the SBV's capacity to defend the USD/VND rate in the face of sustained
capital outflows. Two external risk channels dominate:

**U.S. demand shock.** The United States accounts for roughly one-quarter to one-third of
Vietnam's total exports (electronics, furniture, apparel, footwear). A meaningful U.S.
slowdown would transmit quickly through export volumes, manufacturing employment, household
incomes, and FDI disbursements. Tariff pressures in 2025 reduced Vietnam's price
competitiveness, and value-added embedded in exports to the U.S. amounts to approximately
10% of GDP — among the highest exposure ratios in Asia.

**Japanese rate normalisation.** Rising Japanese interest rates can trigger capital
repatriation and abrupt liquidity withdrawal from frontier and emerging markets including
Vietnam, compounding external funding pressures.

---

## Financial Stability: Growing Deposit–Credit Imbalance

Loan-to-deposit ratios have risen as household and corporate savers have diverted capital
away from traditional bank deposits toward alternative investments — equities, gold, and
digital assets. Pockets of asset quality deterioration are visible in real estate and
construction portfolios. Credit growth remains robust, historically a structural feature
of Vietnam's economic model, but the funding base is narrowing.

## Policy Dilemma: Currency Defense vs. Credit Support

The SBV must simultaneously maintain external confidence through USD/VND stability and
avoid over-tightening an economy that remains structurally dependent on bank credit for
growth. These objectives are in tension. With limited FX reserves reducing the buffer for
outright intervention, the policy mix in 2026 is likely to lean on administrative tools
and selective credit guidance rather than aggressive rate moves.

Forecast scenarios suggest growth could moderate to the mid-5% range if the full-year tariff
impact takes hold and external headwinds intensify — a meaningful step down from the 7%+
trajectory, but still consistent with Vietnam outperforming most regional peers.
