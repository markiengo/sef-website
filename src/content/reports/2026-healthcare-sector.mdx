Healthcare is the rare Vietnamese sector where the bull case needs no forecasting
heroics — it is written into the demographics. A population of more than **100 million**
is ageing and growing wealthier at the same time, while a public hospital system already
running **beyond its design capacity** cannot absorb the demand alone. The arithmetic is
hard to argue with: the market is projected to nearly double, from **$18.5 billion in 2022
to $33.8 billion by 2030** — a 7.8% CAGR — before policy support is even counted.

This report frames the sector across three vectors: the structural demand pulling spending
higher, the pharmaceutical industry the state is deliberately engineering toward
self-sufficiency, and the medical-tourism opportunity that Vietnam's cost advantage is
only beginning to unlock.

---

## A market growing on fundamentals, not hype

Healthcare has been one of the most resilient growth industries in post-Covid Vietnam,
supported by sector privatization, rising consumer demand, and favourable foreign direct
investment. National health expenditure has climbed steadily — and spending *per capita*
faster still — as the state prioritizes the sector in its fiscal agenda. By 2025, healthcare
accounts for roughly **5% of GDP**, and the sector is forecast to grow to twice its size
between 2022 and 2030.

The deeper story is a structural transition. A population north of 100 million is ageing
rapidly even as a widening middle-income bracket demands better care. That combination drives
structurally rising demand for higher-quality medical services, private hospital expansion,
and pharmaceutical consumption — the hallmarks of a long-duration growth industry rather than
a cyclical one.

## A public system at capacity

The demand is colliding with a supply ceiling. Major public hospitals routinely operate at
around **200% of capacity**, and the public system alone cannot close the gap. Policy is
responding: national strategy explicitly steers the private hospital bed share materially
higher by 2030 and toward **25% by 2050**, alongside support for public–private partnership
models. For private operators and the capital backing them, an overloaded public system is
not a problem to solve so much as a structural tailwind to ride.

## The pharmaceutical upside

Pharmaceuticals are the sector's most quantifiable growth engine. The market was valued at
**$6 billion in 2023** and is projected to reach **$8.7 billion by 2028 and $11.6 billion by
2033**, a ~9.7% CAGR. Policy ambition is explicit — targets to meet **80% of domestic drug
demand** locally, lift **local API production to 20%**, and build toward a **$1 billion export**
base — pushing the industry up the value chain via EU-GMP production, localization, and
biosimilar and API manufacturing.

The competitive map is led by **DHG Pharma**, comfortably the largest domestic player by
revenue, followed by **Sanofi Vietnam, Traphaco, Stelapharm, and Imexpharm**. Yet the retail
layer remains strikingly fragmented: independent small pharmacies — an estimated **50,000 of
them** — still handle roughly **85% of the retail market**, leaving consolidation and modern
distribution as a multi-year opportunity in their own right.

## Medical tourism: a cost-advantage story

The least-priced-in leg of the thesis is medical tourism. The market sat at roughly
**$722 million in 2024** and is projected to reach **~$4 billion by 2033** — a ~16% CAGR —
as Vietnam's steep cost advantage versus the United States and regional peers pulls in
inbound demand. The Ministry of Health's 2025–2030 plan to elevate up to **15 hospitals to
international standards** is the supply-side enabler that could turn a price advantage into a
durable destination.

## Outlook

Underserved demand, a supportive and increasingly pro-private policy framework, and
favourable long-run demographics combine to position healthcare as one of Vietnam's most
resilient long-term growth stories. The risks are ones of execution — the pace of
privatization, the fragmentation of retail distribution, and the climb up the pharmaceutical
value chain — not of demand.

> **Thesis:** Underserved demand, supportive regulation, and favourable demographics position
> Vietnamese healthcare for durable, multi-year growth that is structural rather than cyclical.
