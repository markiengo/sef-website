Vietnam enters 2026 with a resilient macroeconomic outlook underpinned by robust growth
momentum and sustained public investment — most visibly in Long Thanh International Airport
and the North–South express railway network. Real GDP expanded **7.09% year-on-year**,
placing Vietnam among the fastest-growing economies in the ASEAN region and well above both
the global average (3.2%) and the ASEAN bloc (4.6%).

**GDP snapshot (2025):** Real GDP US$476.3 billion · GDP per capita US$4,700 · Inflation
3.63% · Unemployment 2.24% · Population 100.9 million

## Export Engine and Its Vulnerabilities

Vietnam's export-oriented model continues to generate sizable current account surpluses.
Manufacturing exports — particularly electronics and high-tech goods — remain the dominant
driver of foreign earnings. FDI inflows of US$38.2 billion (4.2% of GDP) reinforce
Vietnam's position as a regional production hub, concentrated in manufacturing, renewable
energy, and technology-intensive sectors.

However, this strength carries concentrated risk. Roughly one-quarter to one-third of total
exports are destined for the United States, predominantly in electronics, furniture, apparel,
and footwear. A U.S. demand shock would transmit quickly through several mutually reinforcing
channels: export volumes, manufacturing employment, household incomes, FDI disbursements, and
ultimately domestic consumption.

A second external vulnerability sits in the data itself. Significant errors and omissions in
Vietnam's balance-of-payments data — measured at approximately 6.9% of GDP — suggest that
headline external-position indicators may overstate the true strength of the current account.
This complicates policy interpretation and warrants cautious assessment of reported surpluses.

## Banking Sector: Recovery Alongside Persistent Stress

Credit growth remains robust, historically a key engine of Vietnam's economic expansion.
The banking sector is recovering from post-pandemic dislocations, but vulnerabilities
persist: elevated non-performing loans, relatively low capital adequacy metrics, and a
growing deposit–credit imbalance visible in loan-to-deposit ratios. Household and corporate
savers have increasingly diverted capital toward stocks, gold, and digital assets rather than
traditional bank deposits, tightening the funding base for credit extension.

---

## Infrastructure as the Structural Catalyst

The government's fiscal program is anchored in large-scale transport connectivity: Lao
Cai–Hanoi–Hai Phong railway, Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City metro networks, the Long Thanh
International Airport, and two planned nuclear power plants in Ninh Thuan. Estimated
aggregate investment across these projects totals US$185 billion — equivalent to roughly
40% of Vietnam's 2024 GDP. The near-term macroeconomic multipliers are real; the long-run
productivity gains, if execution holds, could structurally shift Vietnam's growth ceiling.

## Conclusion: Structurally Sound, Selectively Exposed

Vietnam's investment case heading into 2026 combines genuine structural advantages — a
competitive labour force, deepening digital economy, accelerating infrastructure, and stable
inflation — with concentrated external exposure and a financial sector still managing
legacy stress. The macro forecast baseline projects continued robust growth; downside
scenarios are driven primarily by U.S. demand deterioration and BoP data revisions that
reduce the apparent current account surplus.
