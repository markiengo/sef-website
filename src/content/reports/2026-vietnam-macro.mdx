Vietnam enters 2026 with a resilient macroeconomic outlook underpinned by robust growth
momentum and sustained public investment — most visibly in Long Thanh International Airport
and the North–South express railway network. Real GDP expanded **7.09% year-on-year**,
placing Vietnam among the fastest-growing economies in the ASEAN region and well above both
the global average (3.2%) and the ASEAN bloc (4.6%). This remarkable performance reflects
the country's successful navigation of post-pandemic recovery, strategic positioning in
global supply chains, and deliberate policy focus on infrastructure development as a
primary growth driver.

The economic landscape is characterized by a pragmatic policy mix that balances growth
objectives with fiscal discipline. While headline indicators paint an optimistic picture,
a deeper analysis reveals structural nuances that warrant careful consideration. The
interplay between export dependence, banking sector health, and infrastructure investment
creates a complex dynamic that will shape Vietnam's trajectory through 2026 and beyond.

---

## Export Engine and Its Vulnerabilities

Vietnam's export-oriented model continues to generate sizable current account surpluses,
serving as the cornerstone of the country's external position. Manufacturing exports —
particularly electronics and high-tech goods — remain the dominant driver of foreign
earnings, with the country having successfully positioned itself as a key node in
global technology supply chains. FDI inflows of US$38.2 billion (4.2% of GDP) reinforce
Vietnam's position as a regional production hub, concentrated in manufacturing, renewable
energy, and technology-intensive sectors. This sustained foreign investment reflects
growing confidence in Vietnam's manufacturing capabilities and strategic location.

However, this strength carries concentrated risk that requires careful monitoring. Roughly
one-quarter to one-third of total exports are destined for the United States, predominantly
in electronics, furniture, apparel, and footwear. This concentration creates a significant
exposure to U.S. economic cycles and consumer demand patterns. A U.S. demand shock would
transmit quickly through several mutually reinforcing channels: export volumes, manufacturing
employment, household incomes, FDI disbursements, and ultimately domestic consumption. The
amplification effect through these channels means that even a moderate slowdown in U.S.
demand could have outsized impacts on Vietnam's growth trajectory.

A second external vulnerability sits in the data itself, presenting a more subtle but
equally important challenge. Significant errors and omissions in Vietnam's balance-of-payments
data — measured at approximately 6.9% of GDP — suggest that headline external-position
indicators may overstate the true strength of the current account. This data gap complicates
policy interpretation and warrants cautious assessment of reported surpluses. The magnitude
of these discrepancies raises questions about the accuracy of external position metrics and
suggests that policymakers may be making decisions based on incomplete information.

## Banking Sector: Recovery Alongside Persistent Stress

Credit growth remains robust, historically a key engine of Vietnam's economic expansion,
reflecting strong demand for financing across both corporate and household sectors. The
banking sector is recovering from post-pandemic dislocations, with improved asset quality
metrics and gradual normalization of lending activities. However, vulnerabilities persist
that could constrain future credit expansion and financial stability: elevated non-performing
loans, relatively low capital adequacy metrics, and a growing deposit–credit imbalance
visible in loan-to-deposit ratios.

These structural challenges are compounded by changing savings behavior among Vietnamese
households and corporations. Household and corporate savers have increasingly diverted
capital toward stocks, gold, and digital assets rather than traditional bank deposits,
tightening the funding base for credit extension. This shift reflects both the search for
higher returns in a low-interest-rate environment and growing financial literacy among
Vietnamese investors. While this diversification is positive from a household wealth
perspective, it creates funding challenges for banks that traditionally relied on stable
deposit bases to support lending activities.

The banking sector's ability to support continued economic growth will depend on its
capacity to address these structural challenges while maintaining credit availability to
productive sectors. Regulatory reforms and capital strengthening initiatives will be
critical to ensuring that the financial system can sustain its role as an engine of economic
expansion.

## Infrastructure as the Structural Catalyst

The government's fiscal program is anchored in large-scale transport connectivity projects
that represent the most ambitious infrastructure development in Vietnam's history. The
portfolio includes the Lao Cai–Hanoi–Hai Phong railway, Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City metro
networks, the Long Thanh International Airport, and two planned nuclear power plants in
Ninh Thuan. These projects are not merely transportation improvements but represent a
comprehensive strategy to reduce logistics costs, improve connectivity between economic
zones, and create the foundation for sustained productivity growth.

Estimated aggregate investment across these projects totals US$185 billion — equivalent to
roughly 40% of Vietnam's 2024 GDP. This scale of investment is unprecedented and reflects
the government's commitment to using infrastructure as a primary driver of economic
transformation. The near-term macroeconomic multipliers are real, with construction activity
generating employment and stimulating demand for materials and services. The long-run
productivity gains, if execution holds, could structurally shift Vietnam's growth ceiling
by reducing transportation costs, improving market access, and enabling new economic
activities that were previously constrained by infrastructure limitations.

determinant of Vietnam's ability to execute its infrastructure vision without creating
financial vulnerabilities.

## Conclusion: Structurally Sound, Selectively Exposed

Vietnam's investment case heading into 2026 combines genuine structural advantages — a
competitive labour force, deepening digital economy, accelerating infrastructure, and stable
inflation — with concentrated external exposure and a financial sector still managing
legacy stress. The macro forecast baseline projects continued robust growth supported by
infrastructure investment and export performance; downside scenarios are driven primarily
by U.S. demand deterioration and BoP data revisions that reduce the apparent current account
surplus.

The structural transformation underway in Vietnam represents one of the most compelling
growth stories in emerging markets, but success is not guaranteed. The country's ability
to navigate external vulnerabilities while strengthening domestic financial institutions
will determine whether 2026 marks another year of outperformance or the beginning of a
more challenging phase. Policymakers face the complex task of maintaining growth momentum
while addressing structural weaknesses that could become more pronounced as the economy
matures.
