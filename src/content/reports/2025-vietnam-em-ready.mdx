Since the launch of Đổi Mới, Vietnam has propelled itself from one of the world's poorest
nations to a dynamic middle-income economy in mere decades. Today, with an FTSE Russell
upgrade to emerging-market status on the horizon, the country is positioned for another step
change. Investor sentiment stays cautious and valuations subdued — but, we'd argue, not for
long.

---

## Liquidity is already showing up

The most tangible sign of readiness is liquidity. With around **700 listed companies**, HOSE
and HNX together posted nearly **US$24.1 billion** in trading value in July 2025 — overtaking
established emerging markets like Malaysia and Indonesia. That depth reflects rising investor
interest fed by strong manufacturing exports and "China + 1" supply-chain shifts, a young and
increasingly tech-driven workforce, and a steadily more supportive legislative environment.

## Valuations still lag the fundamentals

For all that activity, HOSE remains classified as a *frontier* market — a status that has long
deterred foreign capital through friction points like limited language support, capital-gains
tax treatment, and "pre-funding" settlement requirements. These are precisely the barriers the
government has been working to dismantle. A reclassification to emerging-market status could
unlock billions of dollars in foreign net inflows by 2030 as global index funds rebalance
toward Vietnamese equities.

## A consumer economy in the making

Underneath the market plumbing sits a structural demand story. As the middle class expands at
pace, Vietnam is shifting toward a consumption-driven economy in which rising consumerism and
domestic investment increasingly fuel growth — the kind of broadening base that makes an
emerging-market re-rating durable rather than a one-off pop.

So — is Vietnam ready? The liquidity is here, the reforms are underway, and the demographics
are firmly on side. The upgrade is the catalyst; the foundations are already in place.
